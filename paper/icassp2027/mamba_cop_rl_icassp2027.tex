\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a promising approach to
adaptive track management in multi-target direction-of-arrival (DOA)
tracking, replacing hand-tuned thresholds in Gaussian mixture
probability hypothesis density (GM-PHD) filters with a learned policy.
A key open question is whether a recurrent policy with temporal memory
outperforms a memoryless multilayer perceptron (MLP).
We provide a principled, empirically validated answer: \emph{temporal
memory helps when (i) the observation is high-dimensional and raw
rather than hand-summarized, and (ii) the episode is long enough to
create meaningful causal dependencies across scans.}
We instantiate this with \textbf{Mamba-COP-RL}, coupling a selective
state space model (SSM) encoder with a PPO actor-critic.
The policy receives the raw 181-dim COP spatial spectrum at each scan
over episodes of 200 scans.
We introduce \textbf{CombatTrackEnv}, a benchmark with four
combat-realistic scenarios: electronic jamming, stealth target
reappearance, saturation attacks, and formation break-up.
A controlled ablation (12-dim hand-crafted statistics vs.\ 181-dim
raw spectrum) confirms that SSM only outperforms MLP in the
high-dimensional, long-horizon regime.
The full model (42\,K parameters, 41.4\,KB INT8) fits within the
50\,KB SRAM of a STM32H7 Cortex-M7 for real-time edge deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-target DOA tracking with compact arrays is a core challenge
in defense, speech enhancement, and wireless communications.
The COP algorithm \cite{choi2015underdetermined} exploits
fourth-order cumulants to expand $M$ physical sensors to a virtual
array of $\rho(M{-}1){+}1$ elements, resolving up to $K{=}\rho(M{-}1)$
sources---breaking the classical $K \leq M{-}1$ barrier.
Coupling COP with a GM-PHD filter \cite{vo2006gaussian} yields an
end-to-end pipeline for underdetermined multi-target
tracking \cite{choi2026irondome}.

The GM-PHD filter is governed by three scalar hyperparameters:
birth weight $w_b$, pruning threshold $\tau_p$, and detection
probability $p_D$.
Fixed values degrade sharply in non-stationary combat environments:
jamming collapses SNR, stealth targets vanish and reappear, saturation
attacks flood the scene, and formations fragment.
RL offers a data-driven solution---map filter state to parameters
adaptively.

Prior RL-based filter tuning uses memoryless MLP policies
\cite{svensson2014phd_rl,boers2005track_rl}.
A natural extension is to add temporal memory (LSTM, SSM) to the
policy. However, recurrent policies add complexity and can \emph{hurt}
performance if the temporal information in the observation is already
saturated by hand-crafted summary statistics.

\textbf{Central question}: \emph{when does temporal memory help?}

\textbf{Our answer} (Proposition~\ref{prop:ssm_advantage},
Section~\ref{sec:method}): SSM outperforms MLP when the observation
is \emph{high-dimensional raw signal} and the episode is \emph{long}
enough to create multi-scan causal dependencies.
We verify this with a controlled ablation:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{12-dim} hand-crafted statistics (n\_tracks, weights,
    etc.), 40 scans $\Rightarrow$ MLP wins.
  \item \textbf{183-dim} raw COP spectrum, 200 scans
    $\Rightarrow$ Mamba wins on Stealth and Jamming.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Contributions}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A theoretical proposition characterizing when SSM temporal
    memory improves RL-based track management.
  \item \textbf{Mamba-COP-RL}: selective SSM encoder + PPO
    actor-critic; 42\,K params, 41.4\,KB INT8.
  \item \textbf{CombatTrackEnv}: open-source benchmark with four
    combat-realistic scenario generators.
  \item Controlled ablation (12-dim vs.\ 183-dim obs) confirming the
    theoretical prediction.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{RL for multi-target tracking.}
Rosello and Kochenderfer \cite{rosello2018marlmot} modeled track
initiation and termination as MDP actions in a multi-agent RL
framework, but used hand-crafted low-dimensional state features.
Stephan \emph{et al.}\ \cite{stephan2022scene} adapted radar tracker
parameters with DRL using variance-aware rewards; their observation
is also a compact feature vector, consistent with our finding that
MLP suffices in the low-dimensional regime.
Most relevant to our work, Ewers \emph{et al.}\ \cite{ewers2025multitarget}
recently compared sequence-capable architectures (BiGRU,
self-attention) against flat MLP for radar search-and-track RL.
Unlike their work, we focus specifically on the \emph{conditions}
under which temporal memory helps, provide a formal proposition, and
use a selective SSM (Mamba) rather than LSTM/GRU.

\textbf{Recurrent and memory-augmented RL.}
Hausknecht and Stone \cite{hausknecht2015drqn} showed that replacing
a DQN layer with LSTM (DRQN) improves performance in partially
observable environments.
Kapturowski \emph{et al.}\ \cite{kapturowski2019r2d2} scaled recurrent
RL to distributed settings (R2D2) with burn-in for stable hidden
state initialization.
Dorka \cite{dorka2022recurrent} systematically analyzed the
generalization limits of recurrent PPO, motivating our controlled
ablation study.
Unlike LSTM-based policies ($O(d_h^2)$ per step), our Mamba encoder
runs in $O(d_s)$ per scan---critical for embedded edge deployment.

\textbf{Selective SSMs and Mamba.}
Gu \emph{et al.}\ introduced structured SSMs (S4 \cite{gu2022s4},
S5 \cite{smith2023s5}) for long-range sequence modeling, and Mamba
\cite{gu2023mamba} extended them with input-dependent selectivity.
Han \emph{et al.}\ \cite{han2024audiomamba} demonstrated Audio Mamba
for spectrogram-based audio classification, motivating our use of
raw COP spectra as SSM input.
Ota \cite{ota2024decisionmamba} proposed Decision Mamba, replacing
the Transformer backbone in Decision Transformer with Mamba for
offline RL, achieving $28\times$ faster inference.
We are the first to use Mamba as an online RL \emph{temporal encoder}
for adaptive filter parameter tuning in DOA tracking, and the first
to study when such temporal encoding is actually beneficial.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{COP-DOA and GM-PHD Tracking}

The COP pseudo-spectrum is:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{COP}}(\theta) =
  \frac{1}{\|\mat{P}_{\mat{A}}^\perp \vec{a}_v(\theta)\|^2}
  \label{eq:cop_spectrum}
\end{equation}
where $\mat{P}_{\mat{A}}^\perp$ is the noise subspace projector
in the virtual array domain and $\vec{a}_v(\theta)$ is the
virtual steering vector.
Evaluated at $N_\theta{=}181$ angles, $P_{\mathrm{COP}}$ is the
primary observation for our RL policy.

The GM-PHD filter propagates intensity
$\nu_t(\vec{x}) = \sum_j w_j^t \mathcal{N}(\vec{x};\vec{m}_j^t,
\mat{P}_j^t)$ and updates it with COP peak measurements.
Three hyperparameters govern behavior:
$w_b$ (birth weight), $\tau_p$ (prune threshold),
and $p_D$ (detection probability).

\subsection{Selective State Space Models}

A selective SSM \cite{gu2023mamba} defines an input-dependent
recurrence:
\begin{align}
  \mat{A}_t &= \exp(\Delta_t \odot \mat{A}_0),\quad
  \mat{B}_t = \Delta_t \odot \mat{W}_B \vec{x}_t \\
  \vec{h}_t &= \mat{A}_t \vec{h}_{t-1} + \mat{B}_t \vec{x}_t,\quad
  y_t       = \vec{c}_t^\top \vec{h}_t
  \label{eq:ssm}
\end{align}
where $\Delta_t, \mat{B}_t, \vec{c}_t$ are affine functions of input
$\vec{x}_t$.
The key property is \emph{selectivity}: the model learns which
inputs to integrate into hidden state $\vec{h}_t$ and which to
discard, enabling long-range compression without fixed forgetting
rates.

\subsection{Proximal Policy Optimization}

PPO \cite{schulman2017proximal} maximizes:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{CLIP}} = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[
    \min\!\left(r_t A_t,\;
    \mathrm{clip}(r_t, 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon)\, A_t
    \right)
  \right]
  \label{eq:ppo}
\end{equation}
with $r_t {=} \pi_\theta(a_t|o_t)/\pi_{\theta_{\rm old}}$,
$\epsilon{=}0.2$, GAE ($\lambda{=}0.95$), and 4 update epochs
per rollout.

\section{Mamba-COP-RL}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{When Does Temporal Memory Help?}

We formalize the intuition behind our ablation study.

\begin{proposition}[SSM Advantage Condition]
\label{prop:ssm_advantage}
Let $\mathcal{I}(\vec{o}_t; \vec{o}_{1:t-1})$ denote the mutual
information between the current observation and the history.
A temporal encoder (SSM, LSTM) provides advantage over a memoryless
MLP if and only if:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{I}(\vec{o}_t;\, \vec{o}_{1:t-1}) \;\gg\;
  \mathcal{I}(f(\vec{o}_t);\, \vec{o}_{1:t-1})
  \label{eq:prop}
\end{equation}
where $f(\cdot)$ is any hand-crafted summarization of $\vec{o}_t$.
\end{proposition}

Informally: if the raw observation $\vec{o}_t$ contains significantly
more information about the history than a hand-crafted summary
$f(\vec{o}_t)$, a temporal encoder can exploit that information
whereas an MLP operating on $f(\vec{o}_t)$ cannot.

\textbf{Corollary}: For 12-dim statistics (n\_tracks, weight sums,
etc.), $f(\vec{o}_t) \approx \vec{o}_t$ since the statistics already
encode the relevant filter state.
For 183-dim raw spectrum, $f(\vec{o}_t) \ll \vec{o}_t$ since
spectral shape, peak width, and jamming signatures are lost under
scalar summaries---satisfying the SSM advantage condition.


\subsection{MDP Formulation}

At scan $t$, the agent observes $\vec{o}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{183}$,
outputs action $\vec{a}_t \in [-1,1]^3$, and receives reward $r_t$:

\begin{equation}
  \vec{o}_t = \bigl[
    \tilde{P}_{\mathrm{COP}}(\theta_1), \ldots,
    \tilde{P}_{\mathrm{COP}}(\theta_{181}),\;
    t/T,\;\rho_{\mathrm{SNR}}
  \bigr]^\top
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{P}_{\mathrm{COP}}$ is max-normalized,
$\rho_{\mathrm{SNR}} = \min(\hat{P}_{\max}/(10\hat{P}_{10\%}), 1)$
is a peak-to-noise-floor proxy.

Actions map to GM-PHD parameters:
$w_b {\in} [0.02, 0.30]$,
$\tau_p {\in} [10^{-6}, 10^{-3}]$ (log scale),
$p_D {\in} [0.70, 0.99]$.

The reward penalizes tracking error and complexity:
\begin{equation}
  r_t = -\mathrm{GOSPA}(\hat{\Theta}_t, \Theta_t^*)
        - 0.01|\hat{K}_t - K_t^*|
        - 0.001\max(0, J_t - 50)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Architecture}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  % \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_architecture.pdf}
  \fbox{\rule{0pt}{3cm}\rule{\columnwidth}{0pt}}
  \caption{Mamba-COP-RL architecture. The COP spatial spectrum
  (181-dim) passes through the selective SSM encoder, which maintains
  hidden state $\vec{h}_t$ across scans.
  The enriched observation feeds the PPO actor-critic.
  At inference, the SSM operates in single-step mode ($O(d_s)$/scan).}
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows the full pipeline.
The \textbf{SSM encoder} applies:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\vec{o}}_t = \vec{o}_t +
  \mat{W}_{\rm out}\bigl(\vec{g}_t \odot \mathrm{SSM}(
  \mat{W}_{\rm in}\vec{o}_t, \vec{h}_{t-1})\bigr)
  \label{eq:encoder}
\end{equation}
where $\vec{g}_t = \sigma(\mat{W}_g \vec{o}_t)$ is an input gate,
$d_s{=}24$, $d_h{=}48$.
The \textbf{actor-critic} is a two-layer MLP
($183{\to}64{\to}64$, Tanh) with Gaussian actor and linear critic.

\textbf{Key training detail}:
During each PPO update epoch, the full episode sequence
$(\vec{o}_1,\ldots,\vec{o}_T)$ is re-processed through the SSM
encoder (starting from $\vec{h}_0 = \vec{0}$), so that gradients
backpropagate through the temporal encoder.
This differs from naive recurrent PPO implementations that detach
hidden states between updates.

\textbf{Model size}: 42,375 params, 41.4\,KB INT8
($<$ 50\,KB STM32H7 SRAM budget).

\section{CombatTrackEnv}
\label{sec:env}

CombatTrackEnv wraps the COP-PHD tracker as a gym-compatible MDP with
$T{=}200$ scans per episode and SNR ${\sim}\mathcal{U}(5,15)$\,dB.
Table~\ref{tab:scenarios} summarizes the four scenario types.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{CombatTrackEnv scenario types and temporal horizon.
           $\tau_{\rm dep}$ = estimated scan lag of key dependency.}
  \label{tab:scenarios}
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
  \begin{tabular}{lccp{3.2cm}}
    \toprule
    Scenario & $N_{\rm tgt}$ & $\tau_{\rm dep}$ & Key dependency \\
    \midrule
    Jamming    & 2--4 & $>$50 & Pre-jam spectrum for recovery \\
    Stealth    & 2--4 & 15--40 & Trajectory during visibility gap \\
    Saturation & 2--15 & 1--5  & Attack-onset detection \\
    Formation  & 3--5 & 60--130 & Per-target inertia during flight \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Jamming}: Jammer active in $[t_j, t_j{+}\Delta_j]$,
$\Delta_j \in [30,70]$, reducing effective SNR by 10\,dB.

\textbf{Stealth}: Each target independently becomes low-observable
(zero received power) for $\Delta_s \in [15,40]$ scans;
true DOAs continue evolving.
GOSPA counts stealth targets as missed.

\textbf{Saturation}: Baseline of $N_i \in \{2,3\}$ targets;
$N_a \in [6,12]$ additional targets appear at $t_a \in [40,80]$.

\textbf{Formation}: $N_f \in \{3,5\}$ targets in tight 2--4$^\circ$
angular formation; break-up at $t_b \in [60,130]$ with independent
random velocities.

(2)~\textbf{MLP-PPO}: obs\_dim=183, hidden=64 (16,199 params,

(3)~\textbf{Mamba-COP-RL} (described above).

SNR $\sim \mathcal{U}(5,15)$\,dB, Adam optimizer.
MLP-PPO: lr=$3{\times}10^{-4}$;

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We posed and answered the question: \emph{when does temporal memory
help in RL-based track management?}
The answer is when the observation is high-dimensional raw signal
(not hand-summarized) and the episode contains long-range causal
dependencies.
We formalized this as Proposition~\ref{prop:ssm_advantage},
validated it with a controlled ablation (12-dim vs.\ 183-dim),
and instantiated it as Mamba-COP-RL on CombatTrackEnv.
The 41.4\,KB INT8 model enables real-time edge deployment on
Cortex-M7, achieving meaningful gains on Stealth and Jamming
scenarios without any cloud offload.

Future directions: 2-D (azimuth--elevation) DOA extension,
online continual adaptation across operational deployments,
and hardware-in-the-loop validation on STM32H7.
